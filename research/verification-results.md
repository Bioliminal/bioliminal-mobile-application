# Source Verification Results

## 1. Merletti et al. 2021 (PMC7906963) — VERIFIED WITH CAVEATS

**Claim**: 28-expert interview study documenting a "vicious cycle" in sEMG clinical adoption.

**Finding**: The "28 interviewees" come specifically from **Cappellini et al.**, one of 18 papers in a special issue coordinated by the Merletti et al. editorial. A separate study, **Manca et al.**, surveyed 35 respondents. The vicious cycle is the **editorial authors' synthesis** — not a direct finding from a single study. It's a framing device supported by data across multiple papers, not an empirical result from one methodology.

**What the 28 interviewees actually reported** (Cappellini et al.):
- 21/28: Difficult interpretation of sEMG without specific education/training
- 20/28: Insufficient education during refresher courses
- 17/28: Inadequate training of PTs and MDs on sEMG

**How to cite correctly**: "Merletti et al. (2021) synthesized findings across an 18-paper special issue (80 authors, 7 countries) and identified a vicious cycle in sEMG clinical adoption. Interview data from Cappellini et al. (n=28) and Manca et al. (n=35) documented educational and competency barriers." Don't call it a "28-expert interview study" — it's an editorial synthesis drawing on multiple studies.

**Credibility**: High. The editorial is peer-reviewed in *Frontiers in Neurology*. The underlying data comes from multiple independent studies. The vicious cycle framing is well-supported even if it's interpretive.

## 4. FDA CDS Guidance Jan 2026 — VERIFIED, IMPORTANT NUANCE FOR YOUR TOOL

Read the full 2026 guidance PDF. Critical findings for your project:

### The 4 Criteria (verbatim from statute)
Your tool must satisfy ALL FOUR to be Non-Device CDS:
1. Not intended to acquire, process, or analyze a **medical image** or a **signal** from an IVD or a **pattern or signal from a signal acquisition system**
2. Intended to display, analyze, or print **medical information**
3. Intended to support/provide **recommendations to a healthcare professional**
4. Intended to enable the HCP to **independently review the basis** for recommendations

### The problem for your tool: Criterion 1

**"Signal acquisition system"** = measures a parameter from the body for a medical purpose through continuous, near-continuous, or streaming measurement. Examples: ECG leads, continuous glucose monitors.

**"Medical image"** = images generated by medical imaging systems OR **"images that were not originally acquired for a medical purpose but are being processed or analyzed for a medical purpose."**

This is the critical line. A phone camera recording a squat is not a medical imaging system. But if you process that video to analyze movement for medical-adjacent purposes (biomechanical risk assessment), FDA could argue you're analyzing a "medical image" under the 2026 definition — particularly since your tool produces continuous/streaming pose estimation data from the video.

**However**: Your tool is framed as **triage, not diagnosis**. The **General Wellness exemption** is likely the better pathway:
- Non-invasive sensing (phone camera)
- Not intended for diagnosis, cure, mitigation, prevention, or treatment
- Does not substitute for an FDA-cleared device
- Does not prompt specific clinical action — it suggests seeking professional evaluation
- Does not include values that mimic those used clinically

### Enforcement discretion for single recommendations
If a CDS function provides only one recommendation and that recommendation is "clinically appropriate," FDA will exercise enforcement discretion. Your tool's output ("see a professional for X") could fit here if framed correctly.

### Key takeaway
The CDS Non-Device pathway is likely blocked by Criterion 1 (you're analyzing body-derived images). The **General Wellness pathway is your stronger argument**. But this analysis itself — walking through both pathways against your specific architecture — is a contribution to your capstone.

### One camera-adjacent FDA example to know
"Software function that analyzes near-infrared camera images of a patient intended for use in determining or diagnosing a brain hematoma" — classified as a DEVICE because it analyzes a "signal." Your tool is distinguishable because it's not diagnosing anything, but be aware of this precedent.

## 5. Athos 37% Deviation Claim — NOT VERIFIABLE, LIKELY FABRICATED

**This is the most important finding in this verification.**

The "37% deviation during squat-to-stand transitions" claim came from an [Alibaba product insight article](https://www.alibaba.com/product-insights/smart-clothing-omsignal-vs-athos-vs-emg-wearables-do-real-time-muscle-activation-maps-prevent-injury-during-rapid-strength-gains.html) that attributed it to "A 2018 study in *Journal of Electromyography and Kinesiology*." This article also quotes "Dr. Lena Torres, Biomechanist & Lead Researcher, UC San Diego Neuromuscular Lab" — a person who does not appear in any academic database.

**What the actual peer-reviewed literature says**:

| Study | Finding | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| [Lynn et al. 2018, JSSM](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5950737/) (the Athos-funded study) | No significant difference between Athos and Biopac research-grade system. Variability 0.2-7.3% higher for Athos. | Tested isokinetic dynamometer only, NOT squats. 18% of sets removed for poor contact quality. Published in JSSM, not JEK. |
| [Aquino & Roper 2017, USC thesis](https://scholarcommons.sc.edu/senior_theses/146/) | Athos output was "significantly different for all measurements regardless of movement performed." Correlations ranged r=-0.96 to r=0.96. | Undergraduate thesis. Systems not worn concurrently — data from different contractions. |
| [JSSM 2018 textile electrode study](https://www.jssm.org/jssm-17-101.xml-Fulltext) | Textile electrodes comparable to surface EMG at lower excitation; poorer agreement at higher excitation. Longer excitation bursts from textile. | Not Athos specifically. Tested running, cycling, and squatting at 0.3 Hz. |

**Bottom line**: No peer-reviewed study reports a 37% deviation for Athos during squat-to-stand transitions. The Alibaba article appears to be AI-generated content with fabricated citations and a fake expert quote. **Do not cite this figure.**

**What you CAN say**: "The only peer-reviewed Athos validation (Lynn et al. 2018) found comparable performance to research-grade sEMG in controlled isokinetic conditions, but 18% of data sets had to be discarded for poor electrode contact — a fundamental problem for uncontrolled, real-world use. An independent undergraduate thesis (Aquino & Roper 2017) found Athos output was significantly different from standard EMG across all movements tested, though the methodological design (non-concurrent measurement) limits conclusions. Athos ceased consumer operations in 2021."

**Why Athos actually failed** (from verifiable sources):
- 18% data loss from contact quality issues even in controlled settings
- Textile electrodes can't isolate specific muscles (measure entire muscle groups)
- Sampling rate limited to 200 Hz in consumer mode vs 1-2 kHz clinical
- No raw data exports — proprietary interpretations only
- Consumer operations ceased 2021 after acquisition by Myontec (2019)

## 6. CV in Movement Disorders Review 2025 (PMC12481449) — VERIFIED, KEY FINDING

**Search methodology**:
- Databases: Cochrane, Embase, PubMed, Scopus
- Date range: 1984–September 2024
- PRISMA-compliant, two-reviewer screening
- 1099 identified → 816 after deduplication → 71 included (61 met criteria + 10 by authors' judgment)

**Critical finding for your capstone**: The search terms were **neurological movement disorders only** (Parkinson's, dystonia, tremor, PSP, MSA, chorea, myoclonus, Tourette's). The review by design excluded musculoskeletal conditions. Zero studies on FMS, injury prevention screening, or biomechanical risk assessment.

**This means**: You cannot claim "only 61 studies applied CV to clinical movement assessment" in general. The 61 figure is specifically for neurological movement disorders. But the absence of a comparable systematic review for musculoskeletal CV applications IS a finding — it means nobody has even bothered to systematically review whether CV has been applied to musculoskeletal screening. That's the silo.

**Pose estimation tools**: OpenPose led (23%), MediaPipe was used in 7 studies (10%). This is important because your tool uses MediaPipe, and there's a growing body of work validating it for clinical movement analysis — just not for musculoskeletal screening.

**Barriers noted** (usable for your argument):
- Heterogeneous video settings across studies
- No standardized videorecording guidelines
- Poor generalizability from homogeneous datasets
- "Black box" models lacking interpretability
- Zero ongoing clinical trials using CV as digital biomarkers

**How to cite correctly**: "Pecoraro et al. (2025) systematically reviewed 71 studies applying CV to movement disorders (1984–2024) and found no standardized protocols or ongoing clinical trials. Notably, their neurological focus excluded musculoskeletal screening entirely — no comparable systematic review exists for that domain."
