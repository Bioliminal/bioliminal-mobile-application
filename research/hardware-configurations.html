Why Hybrid Cueing: The Six-Dimension Analysis

A practitioner's hand does six things. No single actuator covers all six. This is why we run two types.

Cue DimensionWhat It EncodesVibrationPressure (TSA)
Location"This muscle, not that one"Good (60.7% localization — Deshpande 2021)Slightly worse (54.6%)
Pressure magnitudeAwareness touch → "push into my hand"Binary on/off feelGraduated 0.5–20N
Pressure directionMost important — "press ribs down and in"Radiates omnidirectionallyPartial (skin stretch does this better)

Bottom line: Vibration covers 2/6 dimensions well (location, timing). Pressure covers 4/6 (magnitude, duration, release, partial direction). Neither alone replicates a trainer's hand. The MVP tests both to find what matters most in practice.

Three-State Cueing Gradient

From practitioner behavior — not binary on/off, but a progression

1. Correction

Strong signal — "you're off, shift activation here." Actuator: Vibration burst (fast, salient attention grab) + TSA squeeze onset (sustained directional pressure).

2. Reinforcement

Light sustained signal — "stay here, you've got it." Actuator: TSA at low squeeze (Merkel/Ruffini stay active). Vibration would habituate here — useless for sustained reinforcement.

3. Fade

Signal off — "pattern ingrained, test independence." Actuator: Gradual TSA release. Abrupt release = "did you maintain without the cue?" This is the motor learning transfer test.

Skin Spatial Resolution — The Binding Constraint

The skin can't resolve stimuli as finely as muscles are compartmentalized. Design to the skin's limits, not the muscle's.

Body RegionTwo-Point DiscriminationDistinguishable Cue ZonesImplication
Forearm25–50mm2 zonesBiceps vs brachioradialis distinction — achievable
Upper arm~45mm2 zonesMax 2 independent cue points on biceps/triceps
Thigh/calf30–45mm2–3 zonesVL vs VM distinction on quads — achievable
Torso/back40–60mm2–3 zonesLat vs trap — achievable. Intra-muscle — not reliably
Motor unit physiology note: For biceps, between-muscle distinction (biceps vs brachioradialis) matters more than within-muscle head distinction (long vs short head). For triceps, per-head cueing has stronger justification (Wakahara 2013). However, bicep curl is the better MVP exercise — needs no equipment (dumbbell anywhere), the "stop curling with your forearms" narrative is universally relatable, anterior arm has better electrode contact, and the between-muscle distinction is a larger/easier signal to detect. Save per-head triceps cueing for v2.
Deshpande nuance: Vibrotactile localization was slightly MORE accurate (60.7%) than force localization (54.6%) on the torso. Vibration may be better for "where" and pressure better for "how long / how much." This supports the hybrid approach — use vibration to say WHERE, use pressure to say HOW MUCH and FOR HOW LONG.

sEMG Sensor Options

Emphasis on budget options. The specialist will source specific products — we provide the tech spec.

Budget A — Bare Minimum

DIY Instrumentation Amp

~$3–5 / channel
  • INA128 or AD620 instrumentation amp IC (~$2–4)
  • Passive RC bandpass filter (20–450Hz)
  • Breadboard/perfboard build
  • Disposable Ag/AgCl snap electrodes ($0.20/ea)
  • Analog out to ESP32 ADC
SNR: Noisy — adequate for on/off only • CMRR: ~80dB
Build time: 2–4 hours • Best for: "Does this concept work at all?" testing
Budget B — Best Value MVP

BioAmp EXG Pill

~$8–12 / channel
  • Upside Down Labs — Crowd Supply / Mouser
  • Instrumentation amp, raw biopotential output
  • Open-source hardware (CERN-OHL-S)
  • 25.4 x 10mm, <2g — tiny enough for any wearable
  • Bandwidth 0.5–125Hz default (adjustable to ~500Hz)
SNR: ~5μV noise floor • Output: Analog to any ADC
Best for: Activation level, L/R balance, "wrong muscle first" detection. Our recommended MVP pick
Budget C — Integrated

MyoWare 2.0

~$38–42 / channel
  • SparkFun SEN-21265 — widely stocked
  • Snap-on electrode board — no separate leads needed
  • Envelope + raw EMG mode switch
  • Optional wireless shield (SEN-21460, ESP32-based BLE)
  • 52 x 20mm, ~5g with electrodes
Bandwidth: 25–450Hz • Modes: Rectified envelope or raw
Best for: Fastest path to demo — plug-and-play, extensive Arduino tutorials. More expensive per channel than BioAmp
Mid — Frequency-Domain Capable

ADS1292R Breakout

~$40–50 / 2 channels
  • Protocentral PC-4126 — Mouser / direct
  • 24-bit delta-sigma ADC, 2 differential channels
  • 125–8,000 SPS per channel — full spectral analysis
  • SPI to ESP32/nRF52, 40 x 28mm, ~8g
  • Enables median frequency shift tracking (fatigue/RIR)
CMRR: >100dB • Why upgrade: Unlocks the RIR-confidence feature
Trade-off: More complex integration (SPI vs analog), but half the price of OpenBCI for 2ch

TSA Squeeze Actuator Options

Budget A — Weekend Hack

Micro Servo + Fishing Line

~$5–8 / unit
  • SG90 micro servo (~$2) — position control built-in
  • Monofilament fishing line, 30lb test ($0.25/m)
  • No encoder needed — servo has internal feedback
  • Wrap line around servo horn, route through band
  • PWM from any Arduino pin
Force: 1–3N (weak but perceptible) • Latency: 100–300ms
Dims: 23x12x22mm, 9g • Caveat: Audible whine, low force
Best for: "Can I feel anything at all?" test in an afternoon
Budget B — Proper POC

GA12-N20 Gearmotor + Spectra Line

~$12–18 / unit
  • GA12-N20, 100RPM/6V, 0.3 kg·cm stall (~$3, AliExpress)
  • 80lb Spectra braided line, 0.5mm ($0.50/m)
  • DRV8833 or L9110S H-bridge ($1–2)
  • Current sensing via 0.1Ω shunt resistor as force proxy
  • PTFE tube (1mm ID) string guides sewn into band
Motor: 10x12x24mm, ~10g • Force: 5–15N (clearly perceptible)
Latency: 200–400ms • Fatigue: ~50 cycles (Spectra), swap line as needed
Fail-safe: Motor freewheels on power loss → string untwists passively
Budget C — Reliable Demo

N20 + Dyneema + Hall Encoder

~$20–28 / unit
  • N20 gearmotor with rear shaft ($5) + A3144 hall sensor ($0.50) + small magnet ($0.25)
  • Dyneema braided 0.5mm, 100lb test ($1/m) — >1000 cycles
  • DRV8833 driver ($2) + basic PID in firmware
  • Elastic return band for passive untwist
  • 3D-printed motor housing snap-fit to neoprene band
Force: 5–15N with position feedback • Latency: 150–250ms
Key upgrade over Budget B: Dyneema doesn't fatigue, hall sensor enables repeatable squeeze levels
Best for: A/B testing cue intensity with EMG verification
Mid — Controlled Squeeze

Pololu 25D + Encoder + Dyneema

~$45–55 / unit

Vibrotactile Actuator Options

Budget A — Simplest Possible

Flat Coin ERM (Pager Motor)

~$0.50–1.50 / unit
  • Generic 8-10mm flat coin vibration motor (AliExpress bulk)
  • Drive: GPIO + 2N7002 MOSFET ($0.10)
  • On/off only — no intensity control
  • Self-adhesive backing, stick anywhere
  • 20+ per MCU via direct GPIO
Dims: 8–10mm dia x 2.7–3.4mm • Weight: 0.9–1.2g
Response: 50–100ms spin-up • Current: 60–85mA
Best for: Cheapest possible "does vibration cueing do anything?" test

Precision Microdrives 310-103

~$2–3 / unit
  • 10mm dia x 3.4mm — industry standard coin ERM
  • Alt: Vybronics VG0832014D (8mm, $1.50)
  • Drive via PWM for variable intensity
  • Frequency and amplitude coupled (can't separate)
  • Well-documented datasheets, reliable sourcing
Vibration: ~200Hz, 0.8G amplitude • Current: 75mA
Spacing: 30mm+ on upper arm, 20mm+ on forearm
Best for: Reliable MVP with known specs. Our recommendation for MVP vibrotactile
Mid — Crisp Haptics

LRA + DRV2605L

~$8–15 / unit
  • Jinlong Z-LRA-0825 (8mm, $5–8) — best value LRA
  • DRV2605L I2C haptic driver ($2–3) — auto-resonance
  • 123 preset waveforms + custom registers

Configuration 1: sEMG Only

Sensing without cueing — baseline data collection, activation visualization on phone

sEMG Upper Arm — 2 Channel

Key insight from motor unit physiology: For biceps, electrode placement should distinguish biceps from brachioradialis (different muscles), not long head from short head (same muscle, minimal independent control). For triceps, per-head placement IS justified — consider triceps pushdown as MVP exercise instead.

sEMG Glutes — 3 Channel

3 channels: Glute max, glute med, TFL. Detects "TFL-dominant" pattern — TFL fires before/instead of glute med. Key for Kelsi's movement screening validation.

Configuration 2: sEMG + TSA Squeeze

Two architectures: circumferential band squeeze vs routed contact pads

sEMG TSA Circumferential Band

Band squeeze: TSA motor on lateral arm, string routes through band channels. Squeezes entire circumference uniformly. Simple, but can't target sub-regions. BOM: ~$12–28 for TSA + $16–80 for sEMG.

sEMG TSA Routed Contact Pads

Routed pads: Motors on a wrist/elbow hub, strings route through 1.5mm ID PTFE tubing sewn into compression sleeve. Contact pads are 10-20mm 3D-printed TPU discs with silicone face, pulled into skin against the sleeve. Friction budget: ~10-30% force loss per 90-degree bend (PTFE-on-Dyneema mu ~0.04-0.08). Max practical distance: 30-40cm. Wrist-to-upper-arm (~25cm, one curve) loses ~15-20%. Multi-string from one motor couples forces — need one motor per independent contact point. DIY BOM for 3-channel routed: under $30.

Configuration 3: sEMG + Vibrotactile

Two approaches: spatially separated or time-multiplexed

sEMG Vibe Spatially Separated (2 bands)

Spatial separation: sEMG on muscle belly, vibes on distal arm. Both operate simultaneously. Two separate bands.

sEMG Vibe Time-Multiplexed (1 band)

Time-multiplexed: Co-located on one band. Vibrate pre-rep, pause vibration, sense during rep. Simpler build but loses continuous monitoring.

Configuration 4: Full System (sEMG + TSA + Vibrotactile)

sEMG TSA Vibe Three-Zone Architecture

Three zones on one arm: Zone A = sEMG electrodes under TSA squeeze band on muscle belly. Zone C = vibrotactile array on distal arm (>3cm gap avoids motion artifact). MCU hub near elbow/wrist. TSA squeeze is sustained through the set (slow-adapting receptors). Vibes are discrete pre-rep pulses (fast-adapting). EMG reads continuously — TSA squeeze actually improves electrode contact.

Three Form Factor Approaches

A. Full-Width Band

Pros: Simplest build. TSA string routes through channels. Uniform squeeze improves electrode contact.
Cons: Can't target sub-regions independently.
Best for: MVP single-muscle demo. BOM add: ~$8

B. Discrete Pods

Pros: Repositionable. Each pod self-contained. Scales by adding pods.
Cons: No circumferential squeeze (TSA doesn't work). Adhesive/strap per pod.
Best for: Multi-muscle comparison, Kelsi's validation studies. BOM add: ~$3–5/pod housing

C. Compression Sleeve

Pros: Compression improves electrode contact. String routing channels built in. Single garment.
Cons: Size-specific. Harder to prototype (sewing). Not adjustable.
Best for: Post-MVP polished demo. Base: ~$8–15 off-the-shelf sleeve + modifications

D. Hybrid: Sleeve + Velcro Pods

Pros: Best of both — compression contact + repositionable sensors. Pod positions adjustable per user anatomy.
Cons: Most complex to build.
Best for: If the specialist recommends it — maximum flexibility with good contact. Middle ground approach.

Open Questions for Hardware Specialist

These questions emerged from our research and need specialist input before we finalize the build spec.

Q1 — TSA Form Factor
Circumferential band squeeze vs routed-string contact pads?
Band is simpler but whole-muscle only. Routed strings enable per-region targeting but add friction and complexity. For triceps (where per-head cueing is justified by Wakahara data), routed pads seem worth it. For biceps (where between-muscle matters more than within-muscle), band may be sufficient. What's your recommendation for the MVP?
Q2 — Motor Placement
On-muscle hub vs remote hub with string routing?
Motor on the band (near electrodes) is simpler but adds weight/bulk at the measurement site and motor vibration may introduce artifact. Remote hub (wrist/elbow) with PTFE tube routing is cleaner but friction degrades force. What's the practical max routing distance before friction kills it?
Q3 — Calibration
How should the user set their squeeze pressure threshold?
Options: (a) Fixed squeeze level for everyone, (b) manual dial, (c) auto-calibrate from EMG response — ramp squeeze, detect when EMG RMS changes >10-15% above baseline (staircase algorithm, converges in ~10-20 steps, needs 200-500ms settling per step). Research finding: motor current sensing (~$2, shunt + INA219) is ±10-15% accurate as force proxy during slow squeezes — sufficient for threshold finding. Skip load cell for production; bench-calibrate once. Do you agree motor current is reliable enough here?
Q4 — String Material & Fatigue
Spectra fishing line vs Dyneema braid vs something else for a budget build?
Nylon monofilament stretches and fatigues fast (~50 cycles). Spectra braided is better. Dyneema SK78 is best (>1000 cycles) but overkill for MVP? What's the minimum viable string material that won't fail during a demo? Is pre-stretching necessary?
Q5 — Latency Budget
TSA onset is 150–400ms at budget tier. Is that fast enough?
For pre-rep cueing (before the movement starts): 400ms is fine. For mid-rep correction (user is already lifting wrong): 400ms may be too slow — the rep is over before the cue arrives. The handover doc's Muscle Minder finding suggests pre-rep > continuous, which favors the cheaper/slower TSA. But do we need a mid-rep correction option at all for MVP?
Q6 — Multi-Muscle Scaling
One hub per muscle group, or centralized controller with multiple string outputs?
A single motor can theoretically drive multiple squeeze points via Bowden cable routing, but forces are coupled. A cam or differential spool might give 2:1 independent zones from one motor. Worth the mechanical complexity, or just use separate motors?
Q7 — Compression Sleeve vs Modular Straps
What's easier to prototype and what gives better electrode contact during exercise?
Research finding: Compression sleeve + velcro-mounted pods is the standard approach in research labs (MIT Media Lab wearable haptics). Buy a $5-15 athletic arm sleeve, sew in electrode pockets + PTFE routing channels, attach 3D-printed pods via velcro patches. Compression gives excellent electrode contact; pods are repositionable. Modular bands alone shift too much on sweaty skin. Does this match your experience, or do you see a simpler path?
Q8 — MVP Exercise Selection
Should the MVP demo use triceps pushdown instead of bicep curl?
Research finding: Bicep curl is recommended. Needs zero equipment (dumbbell anywhere, trade show floor). Anterior arm has better landmarks, less variable fat, electrodes stay on convex biceps belly. The "stop curling with your forearms" (biceps vs brachioradialis) narrative is universally relatable. Triceps per-head cueing has stronger physiology backing but needs cable machine and harder electrode placement. Save for v2. Question for specialist: Do you agree from an EMG signal quality perspective? Which exercises give cleanest signal (least motion artifact)?
