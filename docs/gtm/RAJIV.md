# Rajiv Nelakanti

## Role

Product, Cueing Thesis, Go-to-Market

## What I build

The cueing thesis and the product strategy. When to fire a cue, which muscle,
what modality, how to verify it worked, and how to turn every answer into a
data point that makes the next cue better.

The Sense → Reason → Cue → Verify loop is the product. The sensors produce
signal. The ML produces reasoning. The math ensures neither fails silently.
I hold the throughline from signal to intervention to evidence.

## Why it matters

Every EMG wearable in the last ten years was a sensing product that shipped a
dashboard. The category raised real venture money to build beautifully
engineered meters when the job was coaching.

The cueing thesis is the correction: measurement without intervention is not a
product. The system has to act on what it senses, and then verify that the
action worked. That verify step — using the same sensor that triggered the cue
to read whether the muscle actually changed — is where the loop closes. Without
it, you're guessing.

## What I bring

Published game theorist. The cueing system sits on critical math: when is a cue
worth firing? What's the expected cost of a false positive (cueing a muscle
that's fine) vs. a false negative (missing one that's compensating)? How do you
design a fading schedule that builds independence instead of dependency?

These are decision-theory problems, not engineering problems. The engineering
has to be right — and on this team, it is — but the math on top is what makes
the difference between a sensor that measures and a system that coaches.

Market thesis: coaching-quality feedback shouldn't require a $200/hour trainer.
The free tier (camera-only) is the product, not a trial. Premium (sEMG + haptics)
is for people who've felt the value and want the layer cameras can't see.
De-bundling personal training: perceptual judgment, programming, accountability,
human connection — three come apart with sensors and LLMs, one doesn't.
